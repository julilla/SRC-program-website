Students should ... Course level
1.a Understand the extent of data collection and reliance of technologies on large volumes of personal data Intro
1.a.i Grasp the difference and between overt and covert data collection, use, and sharing Intro
1.a.ii Understand the limitation of the distinction between sensitive and non-sensitive data and understand the potential for and danger of re-identification Intro
1.b Understand and articulate value and functions of privacy (including short term and long-term privacy threats) Intro
1.c Be familiar and be able to apply key aspects of major privacy regulations (e.g., CCPA, GDPR, ...) Intermediate, 1000-level
1.d Recognize instances of data collection which enable surveillance and sousveillance (inverse/lateral) surveillance Intermediate, 1000-level
1.e Be able to reason about tradeoffs between privacy and other goods, such as transparency, accountability, safety, or security 1000-level
1.f Understand different conceptions of consent, their potential and limitations as an instrument for data protection Intro
1.g.i Be familiar with widely-used encryption technologies (e.g., public key encryption) and key principles of how they work work Intermediate, 1000-level
1.g.ii Be able to articulate and engage with prominent controversies surrounding encryption 1000-level

Students should ... Course level
2.a.i Understand that representing aspects of the world through data is necessarily reductive and incomplete and that developer perspective shapes the choice and design of variables Intro
2.a.ii Be able to point out variables in datasets which emphasize a particular perspective and identify possible effects of redesigning them Intro, Intermediate
2.b Understand different sources of bias in algorithmic and AI systems (societal vs. dataset vs. system parameters) and their interactions Intro, Intermediate
2.c.i Understand and articulate formal-statistical and substantive conceptions of fairness and justice and understand how they may diverge Intermediate, 1000-level
2.c.ii Make nuanced judgments on tradeoffs between different (formal and substantive) conceptions of algorithmic justice 1000-level
2.d Recognise context-specific limitations of algorithmic and AI decision making and judge when the use of such systems is appropriate and when human decision-making is preferable or required 1000-level
2.e Understand and assess different standards for understanding algorithmic and AI decisions: e.g., transparency, explainability Intermediate, 1000-level
2.f Engage with public-interest-focused applications of AI Intro, Intermediate, 1000-level

Students should ... Course level
3.a Understand and articulate tensions between social pluralism and freedom of expression and user safety and comfort in content moderation Intermediate, 1000-level
